  • Early Life and Education (1904-1925):Nnamdi Azikiwe was born to Igbo parents in Northern Nigeria, where his father worked as a clerk. He attended various schools, including Hope Waddell Training Institute in Calabar and Howard University in the United States. He excelled in his studies and developed a strong interest in journalism and African affairs.
  • Journalism Career and Activism (1920s-1930s): After completing his education in the United States, Azikiwe returned to Nigeria in the late 1920s and became involved in journalism. He founded and edited several newspapers, including the "West African Pilot," which became a platform for him to advocate for African rights, self-governance, and an end to British colonial rule.
  • Political Leadership and Advocacy (1940s-1950s): Azikiwe's political career gained momentum as he joined the National Council of Nigeria and the Cameroons (NCNC), a political party advocating for Nigerian self-governance. He played a crucial role in negotiations with the British government for increased representation and autonomy for Nigerians.
  • Governor-General and President (1960-1966): Nigeria gained independence from British colonial rule on October 1, 1960. Azikiwe became the country's first Governor-General and later its first President. During his presidency, he worked to promote unity among Nigeria's diverse ethnic groups and address economic and social challenges.
  • Later Years and Legacy (1966-1996): Azikiwe's political career continued even after his presidency. He served as a ceremonial president from 1979 to 1983 and remained active in Nigerian politics as a respected elder statesman. He passed away on May 11, 1996, leaving behind a legacy as a founding father of modern Nigeria and a strong advocate for African self-determination and unity.
